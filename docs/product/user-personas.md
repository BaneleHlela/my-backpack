# Who Uses My Backpack?

My Backpack is built for everyone in a South African household who wants to learn — from a 5-year-old hearing IsiZulu vowels for the first time, to a university student cramming before finals, to an adult who simply wants to keep growing. Below are the people My Backpack is designed for.

---

## 1. Banele — The Adult Self-Learner

**Age:** 27  
**Background:** Software developer, self-taught in most things, lives in Johannesburg. Built My Backpack himself because nothing else met his needs. Uses it daily.

**Goals:**
- Expand his English vocabulary and express himself more precisely in writing and conversation
- Stay intellectually engaged outside of work
- Prove that the app he's building actually works for someone like him

**Frustrations with current education:**
- Formal education moves too slowly and is not designed for working adults
- Most vocabulary apps are American-focused and miss nuance
- Existing apps don't feel like they're tracking actual mastery — just streaks and badges

**How he uses My Backpack:**
- Searches for words he encounters in books or conversations
- Adds them to his learning bucket and works through the quiz engine
- Reviews spaced repetition reminders when the app tells him a term is due
- Monitors his confidence scores to see genuine improvement

**What success looks like:**
Banele uses a word correctly in a professional email that he would have struggled to use two months ago. The app's confidence score for that term shows 0.9+. He knows he actually knows it.

---

## 2. Siya — The Young Learner (IsiZulu Sounds)

**Age:** 5  
**Background:** Banele's niece. Grows up in a household where both isiZulu and English are spoken. Starting to learn to read and recognise sounds in both languages.

**Goals:**
- Recognise and produce the five IsiZulu vowel sounds: a, e, i, o, u
- Connect the sound to the written letter and a picture
- Have fun while doing it

**Frustrations with current education:**
- School moves at a group pace — she may be ahead in some areas and behind in others
- She is easily bored by worksheets
- No current tool teaches IsiZulu sounds in a playful, interactive format

**How she uses My Backpack:**
- Listens to the audio for each vowel sound
- Drags the correct letter to match the sound she hears (drag-and-drop questions)
- Earns peanuts for every correct answer — peanuts mean toys
- Uses the app with a parent or older sibling nearby, but can navigate basic screens on her own

**What success looks like:**
Siya correctly identifies all five IsiZulu vowel sounds without help and can write them from memory. She asks to use the app — it is not a chore.

---

## 3. Lebo — The Young Learner (English Beginnings)

**Age:** 5  
**Background:** Banele's other niece. Close friends with Siya. Also at the pre-reading stage. Slightly more drawn to English than IsiZulu, but both matter at home.

**Goals:**
- Learn basic English vocabulary through pictures, audio, and games
- Build letter-sound associations
- Develop a love for reading before it becomes required

**Frustrations with current education:**
- Existing children's apps are American or British — the context doesn't feel familiar
- School worksheets don't engage her the way a game does
- She learns differently to Siya — more visual, less audio-first

**How she uses My Backpack:**
- Works through English vocabulary roadmaps with image-based drag-and-drop questions
- Hears every word pronounced correctly through audio prompts
- Earns peanuts and is motivated by watching her peanut count grow
- Checks progress with a parent at the end of each session

**What success looks like:**
Lebo finishes the first English vocabulary roadmap node, earns her stars, and asks when the next one unlocks. Learning feels like play.

## 4. Mrs Dlamini — The Teacher

**Age:** 42  
**Background:** Grade 3 IsiZulu Home Language teacher at a township school in KwaZulu-Natal. Twenty years of experience. Large classes, few resources. Deeply passionate about her language and her learners.

**Goals:**
- Give her learners access to quality IsiZulu practice outside the classroom
- Align digital content to the CAPS curriculum so it supplements her lessons, not competes with them
- Monitor which learners are struggling without needing to mark 40 tests by hand
- Create content that reflects the specific words and sounds her class is working on

**Frustrations with current education:**
- No digital tools exist for teaching IsiZulu to native speakers at primary level
- She can't monitor individual learner progress in a class of 45
- Quality content creation is time-consuming and she has no support

**How she uses My Backpack:**
- 🔜 Teacher accounts and dashboard are coming in Phase 3
- Once available: logs in to her teacher account, creates roadmap nodes mapped to CAPS
- Assigns content to her class profiles
- Reviews the class progress dashboard to identify learners who need extra support
- Uploads audio recordings of her own voice for IsiZulu pronunciation content

**What success looks like:**
Mrs Dlamini's learners arrive to class having already practised the vowel sounds she will be teaching. Her classroom time is spent deepening understanding, not teaching from scratch.

## 5. Thabo — The High School Overachiever

**Age:** 17  
**Background:** Grade 11 learner at a private school in Pretoria. Studying Mathematics, Physical Sciences, and English. Motivated, self-directed, and frustrated by the pace of his class — he's ready to move on while others catch up.

**Goals:**
- Work through the Grade 11 and Grade 12 Mathematics syllabus ahead of his class
- Be genuinely ready to write his NSC Mathematics exam before the November sitting
- Use his extra time for other pursuits — not just more school

**Frustrations with current education:**
- Class pace is constrained by the slowest learner — he is held back
- His school has no mechanism for early exam sitting
- Existing practice tools don't track whether he is actually ready — just how many questions he's done

**How he uses My Backpack:**
- Works through Mathematics roadmap nodes at his own pace (ahead of class)
- Answers practice questions after each node — the app tells him when he's mastered a topic
- Checks his test-readiness score across all topics in the subject
- When ready, books a slot at a testing centre and writes the assessment on his terms

**What success looks like:**
Thabo writes his NSC Mathematics paper in August, two months before his classmates, and earns an A. He spends September and October on Physical Sciences instead.

---

## 6. Nomsa — The University Student

**Age:** 20  
**Background:** First-year Civil Engineering student at a South African university. Confident in Maths but finds the jump from high school to university-level content difficult. Lectures move fast and she often needs to revisit concepts.

**Goals:**
- Reinforce key Engineering Mathematics concepts through practice
- Fill gaps from high school that are now showing up in lectures
- Have a tool that lets her study on her phone between lectures

**Frustrations with current education:**
- Lectures are not designed for revision — they move forward, always
- Textbooks are expensive and passive — reading doesn't mean understanding
- Khan Academy covers the content but doesn't adapt to what she specifically doesn't know

**How she uses My Backpack:**
- Works through Engineering Mathematics content as it becomes available
- Uses the quiz engine to test herself after a lecture — if she gets questions wrong, she knows what to revise
- Adds vocabulary terms from her Engineering textbooks to her learning bucket
- Reviews confidence scores to see which topics need attention before a test

**What success looks like:**
Nomsa walks into her first-year Engineering Mathematics test feeling genuinely prepared. She knows which topics she mastered and which ones she revised twice. No nasty surprises.

---

## 7. The Parent — The Account Manager

**Age:** 38  
**Background:** Parent of two young children (primary school age). Works full-time. Wants to support their children's learning but doesn't have hours to sit with them every evening.

**Goals:**
- Give the children access to quality educational content in a safe, structured environment
- Make sure the children aren't accessing anything inappropriate
- Be able to see at a glance how each child is progressing
- Control screen time and ensure the app is being used for learning, not entertainment

**Frustrations with current education:**
- Educational apps feel like games with no real learning happening
- No visibility into what the children are actually learning
- Managing separate accounts for each child is a hassle
- Rewards in existing apps are meaningless — children want real things

**How they use My Backpack:**
- Creates the family account and sets up a profile for each child
- Sets a PIN on child profiles so children cannot change settings
- Reviews each child's peanut balance and progress from the owner profile
- Redeems peanuts for real rewards — toys, games, or experiences — at a testing centre or online
- Checks which roadmap nodes each child has completed this week

**What success looks like:**
The parent can see clearly that one child is progressing through IsiZulu sounds and the other is working on English vocabulary. Peanuts are being earned through genuine learning. The children ask to use the app — the parent doesn't have to remind them.
