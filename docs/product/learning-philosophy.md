# How My Backpack Thinks About Learning

My Backpack is not a content library. It is not a quiz app. It is a system that tries to understand how each individual learner learns — and then shapes itself around that person. Every design decision in My Backpack comes from a set of ideas about how learning actually works.

---

## 1. Everyone Learns at Their Own Pace — and That's Okay

The traditional classroom is built around averages. The teacher moves at a pace that works for most learners most of the time. This means fast learners are bored and slow learners are anxious, and neither group is actually being served.

My Backpack doesn't have a class pace. It has your pace.

The app quietly notices when you get things right easily and when you struggle — and adjusts accordingly. If you answer a question correctly with full marks, the app notes that you're confident with that concept and moves on. If you get it wrong, or get partial marks, the app flags that concept and brings it back sooner. Over time, it builds a picture of what you know solidly, what you're still learning, and what you haven't seen yet.

You never need to tell the app how you're doing. It figures it out from watching you work.

---

## 2. Repetition Done Right — Spaced Repetition Explained Simply

Cramming doesn't work. Everyone knows this, but most of us still do it the night before a test. You remember everything in the morning, and within a week you've forgotten most of it. That's not learning — that's short-term memorisation.

The reason cramming fails is that it doesn't give your brain enough time to consolidate what it's learned. Memory is reinforced through retrieval — the act of actively recalling something. And retrieval works best when it happens just as you're starting to forget, not five minutes after you first encounter something.

My Backpack uses a technique called **spaced repetition**: once you've mastered something, the app waits before testing you on it again. The first review comes one day later. Then three days. Then a week. Then two weeks. Then a month. Each time you successfully recall it, the gap gets longer — because you've proven that it's sticking.

Think of it like building a wall. Each time you revisit a concept and get it right, you lay another brick. Skip the revisits, and the wall starts to crumble. Come back at the right intervals, and it stands permanently.

---

## 3. The Same Thing Taught Multiple Ways

Different people remember things differently. Some people learn by hearing. Others by seeing. Others by doing. Most of us learn best through a combination of all three.

My Backpack teaches every concept through multiple question types:

- **Audio** — you hear the word pronounced and choose the correct meaning
- **Drag-and-drop** — you physically place the right answer in the right spot
- **Multiple choice** — you read and reason
- **Typing** — you have to produce the answer from memory, not recognise it
- **Fill-in-the-blank** — you understand words in context, not in isolation

This isn't just variety for its own sake. Different question types target different types of memory and understanding. Recognising a word when you see it is not the same as being able to use it correctly in a sentence. Both matter. My Backpack tests both.

For young children, the emphasis is on audio and drag-and-drop — formats that match how children naturally interact with the world. For adults, all question types are available, including typed input, which requires the deepest form of recall.

---

## 4. Mastery Before Moving On

In My Backpack, you don't unlock the next lesson until you pass the current one. This might feel frustrating if you're in a hurry — but it is one of the most important ideas in the app.

Education systems often let learners move forward without having truly understood the previous step. A child who doesn't understand phonics moves on to reading. A student who doesn't understand algebra moves on to calculus. The gaps accumulate. By the time the gaps become visible, they're enormous and expensive to fix.

My Backpack enforces mastery at every node. You need to reach a passing score before the next node unlocks. This is not about making the app harder — it's about making sure the next thing you learn lands on solid ground.

The payoff is that when you do reach a testing centre, you're genuinely ready — not just "covered the material" ready.

---

## 5. Rewards That Mean Something

Points and badges can feel empty after a while. My Backpack uses a different reward system: **peanuts**, an in-app currency that represents real value.

Peanuts are earned by learning — completing lessons, mastering terms, maintaining streaks, doing well in quizzes. They accumulate in your wallet.

For children, peanuts are redeemed for toys, games, stickers, and experiences — collected at a My Backpack testing centre or redeemed online. For adults, peanuts convert to real money, vouchers, or donations to educational causes.

The psychology behind this is straightforward. Immediate feedback — knowing right now that you earned something — is far more motivating than a distant, abstract reward. Children especially respond to tangible, near-term rewards. But the reward only comes from genuine learning, not from playing around in the app. Peanuts are tied to correct answers, not time spent.

🔜 The peanuts wallet and reward service are designed but not yet built — they are coming in Phase 3.

## 6. Test When You're Ready, Not When the Calendar Says So

One of the most damaging ideas in education is that exams happen at fixed times, on fixed dates, regardless of whether you are ready. A learner who masters the Grade 11 Mathematics syllabus in August should not have to wait until November to write the exam. Equally, a learner who isn't ready in November should not be forced to write anyway.

My Backpack is working towards a different model. The app tracks your mastery across every topic in a subject. When you've genuinely mastered enough material — not just completed it, but retained it over time and across question types — the app alerts you: *You're ready.*

From there, you book a slot at a **testing centre** — a physical location where you write a formal, invigilated assessment. The result is official. You move on. No waiting. No wasted exam fees. No unnecessary failure.

🔜 Test-readiness scoring and testing centres are planned for a future phase. The learning engine that supports them is already being built.
